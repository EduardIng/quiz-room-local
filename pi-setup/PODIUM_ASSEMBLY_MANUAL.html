🏗️ Stand Design

The best quiz experience comes from a tilted lectern-style stand — screen angled ~30° toward the player, buttons at hand height. This lets the player glance at the screen naturally while keeping hands on the buttons, like a game show podium.

Side-profile view of recommended lectern-style podium stand

💡 Stand tip
Mount the Pi behind or inside the stand using M2.5 standoff screws or adhesive velcro pads. Route cables through a hole in the back. A cable gland (€1–2 each, any hardware store) keeps the single power cord entry clean.

🖥️ Monitor 1 — Touchscreen (PlayerView)

This is the main player screen. It must be a touchscreen so players can tap answers directly, in addition to the physical buttons.

Recommended spec: 15.6" IPS, 1920×1080, capacitive touch, HDMI input + USB touch output.

Must-have checklist before buying

  • HDMI input (not just USB-C video) — connects to Pi's micro-HDMI 0
  • USB-A or USB-C touch output — connects to Pi USB port for touch events
  • IPS panel (not TN) — wider viewing angle; players approach from the side
  • Built-in kickstand or VESA mount holes — for stand attachment

Cables needed

  • Micro-HDMI → HDMI cable (from shopping list) — video from Pi to monitor
  • USB-A to USB-A or USB-C cable (usually included in monitor box) — touch data from monitor to Pi

Mounting: Attach a VESA arm or bracket to the stand. Position at ~chest height when the player is standing. Tilt ~25–35° toward the player for comfortable viewing.

⚠️ Touch won't work without the USB cable
The HDMI cable carries video only. Touch input requires a separate USB connection from the monitor to the Pi. This cable is almost always included in the box — check before discarding packaging.

📺 Monitor 2 — Side Display (SideMonitor)

This display shows the player's nickname large enough to read from across the room. It is not a touchscreen — display only.

Recommended spec: Any 24" 1080p IPS HDMI monitor. Brand does not matter — BenQ, AOC, Philips all work. HDMI input required.

Cables needed

  • Micro-HDMI → HDMI cable (from shopping list) — connects Pi's micro-HDMI 1 port to this monitor
  • No USB cable needed — this monitor is display only

Mounting: Mount on the side or back of the stand, facing outward toward other players and the room. Can be mounted vertically (portrait) or horizontally.

💡 Portrait orientation tip
Rotating Monitor 2 to portrait (vertical) mode makes the player's nickname extremely large and readable from across the room, while keeping the monitor footprint narrow on the stand. Portrait rotation is configured in the Pi display settings.

🍓 Raspberry Pi 5 — Board Overview & SD Setup

The Pi 5 is the brain of the podium. Before connecting anything, familiarise yourself with its ports.

Raspberry Pi 5 port layout — ports on left and right edges, GPIO header at top, microSD on underside

Flashing the SD card (on your Mac)

  1. Download Raspberry Pi Imager
    Go to raspberrypi.com/software → Download for macOS. Install and open it.
  2. Insert microSD card into your Mac
    Use the USB card reader from the shopping list. The card will appear in Finder as a drive.
  3. Choose OS in Imager
    Click Choose OS → Raspberry Pi OS (64-bit, Desktop). Do not pick Lite — you need the desktop for Chromium.
  4. Click the gear icon ⚙️ — configure before flashing
    Set hostname: podium-1
    Enable SSH ✓
    Username: pi · Password: choose something and write it down
    Configure WiFi — or skip if using Ethernet (recommended)
    Locale: set to your timezone
  5. Choose Storage → select your microSD card
    Double-check it is the SD card and not your Mac's internal drive.
  6. Click Write and wait ~5 minutes
    Imager will flash and verify automatically. Eject the card when done.
  7. Insert microSD into Pi
    The SD slot is on the underside of the Pi board. Push until it clicks.
🚫 Do not power on the Pi yet
Connect all cables (HDMI, buttons) before applying power. Plugging in HDMI after boot may result in one display not being detected.

🔌 GPIO Button Wiring

GPIO stands for General Purpose Input/Output. The Pi has 40 pins on a header — some provide power, some are GND (ground), and some are programmable signals. We use 4 signal pins and 1 GND pin. No resistors needed — the Pi has built-in pull-up resistors.

Full 40-pin GPIO header. Highlighted pins are the ones you wire to buttons. Locate Pin 1 at the corner nearest the USB-C port.

💡 How to wire a button
Each arcade button has two terminals (legs). Connect one leg to the GPIO pin using a female-to-female dupont wire. Connect the other leg to GND (Pin 6). That's it — press the button and the GPIO pin reads LOW, which the software interprets as a button press.

Each button needs exactly 2 wires: one to its GPIO pin, one to GND (Pin 6). All 4 buttons can share the same GND pin.

🔧 Cable Routing & Final Assembly

Connect everything in this order before applying power.

  1. Mount the Pi inside / behind the stand
    Use M2.5 standoff screws or adhesive velcro pads. Do not let the Pi board touch bare metal.
  2. Route micro-HDMI 0 cable: Pi → Monitor 1
    Keep cable length ≤1 m. Use the micro-HDMI end at the Pi and the full-size HDMI end at the monitor.
  3. Route micro-HDMI 1 cable: Pi → Monitor 2
    Same as above — micro-HDMI to the Pi's second port, HDMI to Monitor 2.
  4. Route USB-A cable: Pi → Monitor 1 (touch input)
    This enables touch. Connect from any USB-A port on the Pi to the monitor's USB input (usually marked with a touch icon).
  5. Connect Ethernet cable: Pi → network switch
    Run the cable from the Pi's RJ-45 port to the LAN switch or wall socket. Wired is more reliable than WiFi for a kiosk.
  6. Wire the GPIO buttons (see wiring table above)
    A=Pin11, B=Pin13, C=Pin15, D=Pin16. All share GND on Pin 6. Use female-to-female dupont wires.
  7. Plug in USB-C PSU — power on
    The Pi powers on automatically when the PSU is connected. There is no power button.

Pre-power checklist

  • microSD card inserted in Pi (underside slot)
  • micro-HDMI 0 cable connected Pi → Monitor 1
  • micro-HDMI 1 cable connected Pi → Monitor 2
  • USB cable connected Pi → Monitor 1 (touch)
  • Ethernet cable connected Pi → network switch
  • Button A (Red) wired: Pin 11 → one terminal, Pin 6 → other terminal
  • Button B (Blue) wired: Pin 13 → one terminal, Pin 6 → other terminal
  • Button C (Green) wired: Pin 15 → one terminal, Pin 6 → other terminal
  • Button D (Orange) wired: Pin 16 → one terminal, Pin 6 → other terminal
  • All cables secured with velcro ties, labelled at both ends
  • Nothing touching the Pi board directly (use standoffs or velcro)
  • Power supply plugged in last → Pi starts immediately (no on/off switch)
💡 Cable management tips
Use velcro cable ties (not zip ties — easier to adjust). Label each cable at both ends with tape. Route HDMI and power cables on opposite sides. Leave 10 cm slack at each connector.
